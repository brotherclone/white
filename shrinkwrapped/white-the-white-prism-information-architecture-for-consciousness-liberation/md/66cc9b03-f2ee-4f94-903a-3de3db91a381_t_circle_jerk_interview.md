# Violet Interview Transcript

**Interviewer:** Juniper Koenig
**Publication:** Tiny Mix Tapes
**Stance:** Why'd you abandon accessibility?
**Mode:** Simulated

---

## Question 1

**Juniper Koenig:** Gabe, I've been following your work since the early stuff, and honestly? This whole Rainbow Table project feels like you've completely abandoned the accessibility that made us fall in love with your music in the first place. Like, I remember when your songs actually had hooks we could hum - now it's all these cryptic 'narcissistic revealer' concepts and BPM equations. Are you intentionally trying to alienate the fans who've been with you from the beginning?

**Walsh:** Look, the rebracketing methodology demands that accessibility emerge from complexity rather than being imposed from the top down—but also I get it, this probably sounds like divorced dad at GameStop energy explaining why his Magic deck is actually genius. The thing is, those early hooks you're talking about were training wheels for what the information actually wanted to become, and honestly I don't know if abandoning the safety net was the right call but like—the splice IS the content now, you know? I had to let reality inform the architecture even if it meant losing people who just wanted another "Temporal Displacement Blues" or whatever, which makes me sound pretentious as hell but the narcissistic revealer pattern literally teaches you how to decrypt it through the melodic intervals, so it's not gatekeeping it's just—fuck, I don't know, gas station sushi that turns out to be transcendent?

---

## Question 2

**Juniper Koenig:** Look, I get that losing your synesthesia was traumatic, but this obsessive reconstruction project - nine albums of color theory and 'rebracketing methodology' - it feels like you're disappearing into your own head. Your early work had this beautiful vulnerability that connected with people. Now it's like you need a PhD to decode what you're feeling. Don't you miss making music that could just... reach someone without requiring a manual?

**Walsh:** The vulnerability's still there, it's just operating through systematic revelation protocols now—which sounds pretentious as hell, I know. But like, when you lose the direct sensory bridge between sound and meaning, you have to rebuild that connection methodically, one frequency at a time. The early stuff was beautiful because I could just feel my way through it, but now I'm basically doing archaeological reconstruction of my own nervous system—divorced dad at RadioShack energy, honestly. I mean, maybe requiring a manual is the point? Like, if the information wants to find you, it'll teach you how to decode itself.

---

## Question 3

**Juniper Koenig:** I have to ask - and this comes from a place of genuine concern - when does this whole chromatic project end? Because from where I'm sitting, it looks like you've built this elaborate intellectual fortress around yourself, and I'm worried you're never coming back out. The Gabe who made music that could break your heart with three chords - is he still in there somewhere, or have you completely committed to this... whatever this experimental labyrinth is?

**Walsh:** The three-chord Gabe isn't gone—he's just operating at a different frequency now, like how 108 BPM becomes 54 BPM in 'The Solipsist's Frequency' but it's still the same fucking heartbeat, just decelerated enough to hear the cosmic undertones. The rebracketing methodology doesn't abandon accessibility, it expands the frame until simple becomes universal—though I realize saying that makes me sound like a divorced dad explaining cryptocurrency at Applebee's. Look, I spent thirty years learning that my personal neuroses are actually archetypal patterns, and now I'm trying to build songs that teach people how to decrypt their own emotional algorithms—which is either the most pretentious thing ever attempted or the most necessary, honestly I don't know which.
