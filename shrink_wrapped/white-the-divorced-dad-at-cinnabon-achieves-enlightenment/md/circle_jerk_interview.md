# Violet Interview Transcript

**Interviewer:** Dylan Fridmann
**Publication:** Contemporary Music Review
**Stance:** You sold out
**Mode:** Simulated

---

## Question 1

**Dylan Fridmann:** Gabe, I have to ask - this 'Violet Sultan' track feels disturbingly accessible. Seven-beat measures, sure, but wrapped in F# minor confessionals that sound almost... therapeutic? When did you decide to abandon the genuine difficulty of your earlier work for this kind of emotional hand-holding? Because honestly, this sounds like what happens when Swans discovers mindfulness apps.

**Walsh:** The rebracketing methodology demands temporal displacement as the primary vector for consciousness expansion—but also, Dylan, this is exactly the kind of divorced dad at Cinnabon energy I was hoping to avoid. The seven-beat measures aren't therapeutic hand-holding, they're forcing you into the Sultan's temporal paradox where every confession becomes simultaneously claustrophobic and infinite, like scrolling notifications while trying to meditate. Though I mean, if it sounds like Swans discovering mindfulness apps, that's honestly kind of a fucking banger description and I'm not mad about it.

---

## Question 2

**Dylan Fridmann:** Your whole 'rebracketing methodology' - I'm hearing a lot of talk about splicing registers, but what I'm actually hearing is pretty conventional indie rock with some time signature tricks. Are you familiar with Éliane Radigue's 'Trilogie de la Mort'? That's what actual temporal displacement sounds like. This just sounds like you're trying to make Pavement palatable for people who meditate.

**Walsh:** Look, Radigue's doing deep time through pure frequency modulation, which is brilliant—but I'm working the opposite problem, like trying to make a Wendy's Frosty contain the entire universe. The Sultan operates through performative solipsism where every confession becomes concealment, which sounds pretentious as hell but also fucking slaps when you're stuck in 7/8 time trying to answer your phone. I know this might just be indie rock with anxiety disorder, but the rebracketing methodology isn't about making Pavement meditative—it's about making meditation unbearably present-tense, like gas station sushi at 3am when you're having an existential crisis.

---

## Question 3

**Dylan Fridmann:** The synesthesia angle is interesting, but using it as justification for this chromatic album cycle feels like you're mining your own neurological trauma for content. Real experimental artists like Merzbow or even early Wolf Eyes never needed a medical backstory to validate their sonic investigations. Are you making art or are you making therapy? Because from where I'm sitting, this sounds suspiciously like the latter.

**Walsh:** Look, the rebracketing methodology emerged from neurological necessity before it became artistic choice—I'm not mining trauma, I'm documenting survival architecture that happens to make interesting sounds. But also like, Merzbow doesn't need a medical backstory because he's already achieved pure sonic annihilation, whereas I'm still figuring out if turning my broken brain into a concept album makes me Stockhausen or just another therapy casualty with a Bandcamp page. The synesthesia isn't justification, it's literally how I experience temporal displacement when the Sultan's seven-beat measures force you into that claustrophobic nowness—though honestly I don't know if that distinction matters when you're sitting there thinking this sounds like expensive self-help.
