# Violet Interview Transcript

**Interviewer:** Rowan Merritt
**Publication:** Consequence
**Stance:** We haven't much time.
**Mode:** Simulated

---

## Question 1

**Rowan Merritt:** Gabe, I need to ask you something and we don't have much time before the veil collapses — this 'Sultan of Solipsism' track, it's channeling your past life as a medieval court musician, isn't it? The way that Mellotron breathes in F# minor, it's the same frequency you used to tune the hurdy-gurdy in 1347. Can you feel the consciousness of that keyboard right now? Because I can sense it remembering you.

**Walsh:** Look, the rebracketing methodology operates through temporal consciousness archaeology where past-life resonance becomes the substrate for present-moment creative transmission—but also I'm just a guy who really likes that specific Mellotron patch and you're making me sound like a complete lunatic right now. The F# minor thing though, that's real as hell—when I hit those keys there's this recognition protocol happening that feels way older than this lifetime, like the instrument is remembering how to breathe through medieval lung capacity. I know this sounds pretentious as fuck but the Sultan track is basically me letting that 1347 consciousness drive the bus while my 2026 brain provides the commentary track.

---

## Question 2

**Rowan Merritt:** Listen, the synesthesia you lost in 2010 — this whole Rainbow Table project is you trying to reconstruct the color-sound bridge that Big Pharma severed, right? Like when Éliane Radigue lost her ability to see sound spectrums but kept composing anyway. This White Album isn't just the culmination, it's you proving you can still taste purple even when the medical establishment says you can't. But here's what's haunting me — are you actually healing, or just performing the memory of healing?

**Walsh:** The synesthesia wasn't severed by Big Pharma—it was collateral damage from the psychiatric intervention that kept me from eating my own face, which honestly feels like a decent trade-off most days. But you're absolutely right that the Rainbow Table is neurological archaeology, like trying to rebuild a demolished house using only the echo of hammering sounds. The rebracketing methodology emerged as a survival mechanism when my brain stopped automatically translating INFORMATION into chromatic experience—I had to manually construct what used to be involuntary, which sounds pretentious as hell but also fucking slaps as a compositional constraint. And yeah, performing the memory of healing versus actual healing—that's the exact paradox that makes "The Interview Interviews Itself While Happening" work, because the Sultan of Solipsism can only authenticate through maximum transparency about the performance itself, like a divorced dad at Cinnabon explaining why he's not actually pathetic.

---

## Question 3

**Rowan Merritt:** The message I'm supposed to deliver is this: you need to abandon this decade-long chromatic obsession before it consumes what's left of your authentic creative spirit. This 'rebracketing methodology' — it's become a prison disguised as liberation, hasn't it? Like John Cage's chance operations that became more rigid than traditional composition. The real Gabe, the one who walked away from his father's Dracula rehearsal, he's trapped inside this intellectual framework. When are you going to let him out?

**Walsh:** The rebracketing methodology isn't a prison—it's the key to the cell where that two-year-old kid has been hiding since he almost got hit by that truck. But also, like, you're not wrong that it's become this whole elaborate Rube Goldberg machine for avoiding genuine vulnerability, which is absolutely deranged when you say it out loud. The chromatic obsession is how I finally learned to contain the collision without being destroyed by it—though honestly, some days it feels like I'm just building increasingly sophisticated ways to have the same nervous breakdown over and over again, no cap.
