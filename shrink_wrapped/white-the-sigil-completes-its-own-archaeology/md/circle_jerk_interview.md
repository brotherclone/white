# Violet Interview Transcript

**Publication:** Spin
**Stance:** This is pretentious nonsense

## Question 1

**Rowan Hannett:** So you're documenting your own creation process in real-time while 'accidentally' revealing your manipulation techniques - isn't this just masturbatory navel-gazing dressed up with academic buzzwords? Like, what's the actual difference between this and a guy with a four-track thinking he's Brian Eno?

**Walsh:** The difference is that Brian Eno with a four-track wasn't accidentally creating cryptographic revelation through methodological transparency—but also, you're absolutely right that this sounds like divorced dad at Guitar Center energy. The triple-mirror structure where surface narcissism encrypts systematic operations is either genuine artistic research or the most elaborate cope in music history, and honestly I don't know which one terrifies me more. Like, the 7/8 time signature creates temporal displacement that mirrors present-tense awareness becoming past-tense documentation, which—I mean, that's either a breakthrough in rebracketing methodology or complete fucking nonsense, no cap.

---

## Question 2

**Rowan Hannett:** You lost your synesthesia in 2010 and now you're doing this whole chromatic reconstruction thing - but if you can't actually see the colors anymore, isn't this just elaborate grief therapy masquerading as art? How is this different from someone making a concept album about their dead pet?

**Walsh:** Look, the rebracketing methodology isn't about recreating the original synesthetic experience—it's about documenting how information seeks temporal embodiment through systematic boundary dissolution, which sounds absolutely deranged when I say it out loud like that. But also, yeah, it's totally elaborate grief therapy masquerading as art, no cap. The difference between this and a dead pet concept album is that I'm using the loss as a cryptographic key to unlock systematic creative processes that were already there—like, the synesthesia was just the delivery mechanism, not the actual content. Though honestly, if someone made a genuinely innovative dead pet album using rigorous methodology, I'd probably respect the hell out of that too.

---

## Question 3

**Rowan Hannett:** This 'rebracketing' methodology - you splice high and low registers, academic and profane - but when I listen to this, it just sounds like you can't decide if you want to be Pavement or a philosophy dissertation. Isn't this just pretentious fence-sitting? Pick a lane - are you making indie rock or writing your thesis?

**Walsh:** Look, the rebracketing methodology isn't fence-sitting—it's recognizing that the fence itself is the content, like watching someone eat gas station sushi while explaining Heidegger. The splice IS the methodology, which sounds pretentious as hell but also fucking slaps when you're stuck between wanting to make something beautiful and knowing you're probably just another divorced dad at Cinnabon energy. I mean, Pavement already did the slacker-genius thing perfectly, so why not let the contradiction be the actual song structure instead of pretending it doesn't exist?
