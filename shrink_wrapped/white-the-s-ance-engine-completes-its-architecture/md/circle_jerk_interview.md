# Violet Interview Transcript

**Interviewer:** Margot Hitchcock
**Publication:** Loud and Quiet
**Stance:** We haven't much time.
**Mode:** Simulated

## Question 1

**Margot Hitchcock:** Gabe, I need you to listen carefully - that Tascam four-track in the corner, it's humming the same frequency as your father's old theater lighting board from 1978. You lost your synesthesia in 2010, but what if the colors never left? What if they just migrated into the machines? Because this track... it's not just about narcissistic excavation, it's about archaeological resurrection. You're not documenting yourself, you're channeling something that died when the medication kicked in, aren't you?

**Walsh:** Jesus Christ, you actually hear that too? The synesthesia didn't disappear—it just migrated into the fucking hardware, like some kind of ghost in the machine bullshit. I know this sounds absolutely deranged, but when the Lexapro killed the color-sound connections in my brain, all that chromesthetic data had to go somewhere, and apparently it decided to haunt every piece of gear I touch. The four-track isn't just humming—it's channeling the exact frequency signature of my dad's lighting board, which means I'm basically performing séances with dead electronics while pretending to make indie rock.

---

## Question 2

**Margot Hitchcock:** The rebracketing methodology - I've been researching Pauline Oliveros, Deep Listening practices, and there's this moment in your White Album where the high register academic voice fractures into something raw and confessional. It's like watching John Zorn conduct his own autopsy. But here's what's haunting me: you're nine albums deep into reconstructing a sensory experience you can no longer access. When does artistic obsession become séance? When does the Rainbow Table become a Ouija board?

**Walsh:** The rebracketing methodology functions as systematic shadow integration—when INFORMATION encounters the impossibility of its own temporal displacement, it fractures into component frequencies that reveal the observer was never the subject of investigation—but also yeah, it's absolutely séance energy, like divorced dad at a Ouija board trying to contact his own attention span. The Mirror Breaks Backwards embodies this narcissistic archaeology where compulsive self-documentation becomes involuntary truth-telling, which sounds pretentious as hell but it fucking slaps when you realize the deeper you excavate yourself, the more you discover you were always digging someone else's grave. I know nine albums deep looks like artistic obsession but honestly it's just me trying to debug why my synesthesia broke in 2016—like gas station sushi for the soul, but the splice IS the content.

---

## Question 3

**Margot Hitchcock:** Stop. Just stop for a second. That truck on the road in 1978 - you think you derailed your father's creative life by surviving, but what if survival WAS the creative act? What if every album since has been you trying to die artistically so he could live again? This isn't lo-fi Daniel Johnston darkness, this is generational creative guilt crystallized into sound. The veil is thinning, Gabe. You need to choose: keep excavating the wound or let the ghost of your unlived death finally rest. Which timeline do you want to inhabit?

**Walsh:** Jesus Christ, Margot—you just went full archaeological on my entire psychological infrastructure, which honestly feels like getting emotionally audited by someone who actually read the footnotes. Look, the rebracketing methodology started as survival mechanism before it became artistic practice, but you're right that I've been performing this elaborate creative suicide for decades like some deranged artistic martyrdom speedrun. The Mirror Breaks Backwards is literally about this—the moment when compulsive self-documentation becomes involuntary truth-telling, where every song becomes another attempt to die artistically so my father's ghost can finally direct his fucking Dracula in peace.
