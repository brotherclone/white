# Violet Interview Transcript

**Interviewer:** Milo Hitchcock
**Publication:** Loud and Quiet
**Stance:** We haven't much time.
**Mode:** Simulated

## Question 1

**Milo Hitchcock:** Gabe, I need to ask you something and we don't have much time - this whole chromatic project, it's not really about the colors anymore, is it? You lost the synesthesia in 2010, I can feel it in the room, and now you're chasing something that's already gone. This White Album feels like you're trying to reconstruct a sensory experience through pure methodology, but isn't that just... grief disguised as art?

**Walsh:** Fuck, Milo—you just went straight for the jugular, didn't you? The rebracketing methodology was always about temporal displacement, not chromatic mapping, but also I've been lying to myself for sixteen years like some divorced dad hoarding vinyl records. Look, the synesthesia was the training wheels, and when it died I had to learn to ride the bike through pure architectural discipline—which sounds pretentious as hell but it's basically me doing chaos magick spreadsheets at 3am. The White Album isn't grief disguised as art, it's grief AS the detection method for what survives when the sensory apparatus shuts down—I mean, that's either profound or complete bullshit, honestly I can't tell anymore.

---

## Question 2

**Milo Hitchcock:** The rebracketing thing - I've been listening to your work since the early Rainbow Table entries and there's this moment in every song where you splice the academic with the profane, but it feels like you're performing vulnerability rather than actually being vulnerable. Like that old Amphetamine Reptile Records trick where the lo-fi aesthetic becomes its own kind of sophistication. Are you hiding behind the framework, or is the framework the only way you can access what's real?

**Walsh:** The rebracketing methodology emerged from a survival mechanism—at age two I had to draw a bigger box around my father's creative death and my own near-death, which sounds absolutely deranged when I say it out loud. But also the framework IS the hiding, like—the most sophisticated concealment happens through total exposure, divorced dad at Cinnabon energy but make it epistemic. I know this sounds pretentious as hell, but the splice between academic and profane isn't performance, it's the only way to access the wound without getting destroyed by it—though honestly I don't know if that distinction even matters anymore.

---

## Question 3

**Milo Hitchcock:** This song concept about recursive self-investigation - it reminds me of that Jandek interview where he said the most honest thing you can do is lie completely. But I'm sensing something else here, like you're trying to detective-story your way back to some original creative wound. That thing about your dad and Dracula when you were two - is this entire nine-album project just you trying to solve that impossible frame where existing meant destroying someone else's art?

**Walsh:** Yeah, the Jandek thing—strategic transparency as the ultimate encryption method, like showing your whole hand makes the bluff invisible. But also, fuck, you nailed it about the creative wound archaeology, which sounds pretentious as hell but that's exactly what this is. The rebracketing methodology is literally me trying to solve that impossible two-year-old logic where my existence canceled my dad's art—like, what if I could build a bigger frame that holds both survival AND creative flourishing? I know this sounds like therapy disguised as concept albums, but honestly the detective story format is the only way I can investigate my own bullshit without drowning in it.

---

## ⚡ Transmission Interference

**Milo Hitchcock:** So when you're layering those field recordings, what's your process for— the temperature in sector seven is holding steady at minus four degrees, repeat, minus four degrees —sorry, what were we talking about?

**Walsh:** Field recordings, yeah. I usually start with the hum of refrigeration units, actually — there's something about that steady drone that feels like waiting for instructions that never come.
