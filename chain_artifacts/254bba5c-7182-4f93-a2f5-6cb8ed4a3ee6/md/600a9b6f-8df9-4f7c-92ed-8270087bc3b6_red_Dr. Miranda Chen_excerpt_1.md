Chapter 1: The Great Disconnection

The analog world that Egan mourned was never as pure as he imagined. His romanticized vision of pre-digital existence—all handwritten letters and face-to-face conversations—conveniently ignores the isolation, the barriers, the voices that were never heard because they lacked access to the printing press, the radio tower, the television studio.

I write these words on a machine that connects me instantly to millions of minds across the globe. Each keystroke is both deeply personal and inherently networked. This is not the death of authenticity that Egan feared; it is authenticity multiplied, democratized, set free from the gatekeepers who once decided whose thoughts deserved preservation.

Consider the teenager in rural Montana who discovers her identity through an online community of artists. Consider the elderly man in Tokyo who learns to paint through YouTube tutorials created by a woman in São Paulo. Consider the revolution organized through text messages, the scientific breakthrough shared through email, the love letter sent as pixels across an ocean.

These connections are not lesser than their analog predecessors. They are not shadows of "real" relationships. They are new forms of human connection, as valid and vital as any that came before. To dismiss them as Egan did is to dismiss the lived experiences of billions of people who have found meaning, purpose, and community in digital spaces.

The great disconnection is not from our devices. It is from those who refuse to see the profound humanity in our networked age.
