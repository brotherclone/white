The most telling example of flawed methodology in thoughtform research comes from Steiner's own experimental protocols, detailed in Chapter 9 of his work. In what he calls the "Stanford Thoughtform Transmission Experiment," Steiner claims to have demonstrated measurable electromagnetic effects from concentrated meditation sessions.

The experimental setup, as described, involved placing sensitive magnetometers and spectrum analyzers in a Faraday cage while test subjects attempted to "project thoughtforms" from an adjacent room. Steiner reports detecting "anomalous electromagnetic signatures" during periods of intense concentration, particularly when subjects visualized geometric patterns or focused on emotional states.

However, a careful analysis of Steiner's methodology reveals numerous critical flaws that render his conclusions scientifically meaningless:

1) Lack of proper controls: No baseline measurements were taken during periods when subjects were not attempting thoughtform projection. Any electromagnetic anomalies could easily be attributed to external sourcesâ€”radio transmissions, electrical equipment, or even the building's HVAC system.

2) Observer bias: Steiner himself operated the detection equipment and interpreted the readings. There was no double-blind protocol to prevent unconscious influence on data collection or analysis.

3) Statistical insignificance: The reported "anomalies" fell well within the normal range of electromagnetic background noise. Steiner presents no statistical analysis demonstrating that his results exceed chance probability.

4) Failure to replicate: When Dr. James Morrison at MIT attempted to reproduce Steiner's experiment using identical equipment and protocols, no anomalous readings were detected across 200 trial sessions.
